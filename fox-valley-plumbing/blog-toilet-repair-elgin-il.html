The Most Common Toilet Problems (And Whether They're Worth Fixing)

Before you panic or reach for a credit card, it helps to know what you're actually dealing with. Most toilet problems fall into a handful of predictable categories, and many of them are straightforward repairs.

Running Toilet

A running toilet is one of the sneakiest plumbing problems in any home. It rarely floods, so people ignore it — but a toilet that runs constantly can waste up to 200 gallons of water every single day. That's $50 or more added to your monthly water bill without you even noticing.

The good news? running toilet repair is usually simple and inexpensive. Nine times out of ten, the culprit is a worn flapper valve or a faulty fill valve — both are cheap parts that a plumber can swap out in under an hour. This is a clear "repair" situation.

Weak or Incomplete Flush

If your toilet needs two flushes to do the job of one, the issue is typically a clogged rim jet, a low water level in the tank, or a partially blocked drain. All of these are repairable. Clogged rim jets can be cleared chemically or manually, and water level adjustments take minutes.

Toilet That Rocks or Leaks at the Base

A toilet that wobbles or shows water pooling around the base has a failed wax ring seal. Left alone, this will rot your subfloor and potentially cause mold. A wax ring replacement costs $150–$300 with labor — a worthwhile repair if the toilet itself is in good shape.

Cracked Tank or Bowl

This one is different. A crack in the porcelain — even a hairline crack — is a red flag. Porcelain doesn't repair reliably, and an ongoing slow leak will damage flooring over time. If the crack is in the bowl itself, replacement is the only sound option.

How Toilet Age and Water Efficiency Factor Into the Decision

Older toilets can use 3–7 gallons per flush vs. 1.28 GPF for today's WaterSense models.

Here's something a lot of homeowners don't think about: even if your old toilet isn't broken, it might be costing you money every single month.

Toilets manufactured before 1994 use 3.5 to 7 gallons per flush (GPF). Modern EPA WaterSense-certified toilets use just 1.28 GPF — that's a savings of up to 60% on toilet-related water use. For a family of four, that can add up to $100+ per year.

If your toilet was installed before the turn of the millennium and you're already dealing with repair costs, that's a strong signal to replace rather than sink more money into an inefficient fixture.

The Age Rule of Thumb

  • Under 10 years old: Repair is almost always the right move. Parts are available and the fixture has plenty of life left.
  • 10–20 years old: Evaluate the repair cost. If you're looking at more than $200–$300 in repairs, replacement starts to make financial sense.
  • Over 20 years old: Unless the toilet is in flawless condition, replacement usually wins. You'll recoup the cost in water savings and avoid a cascade of future repairs.

When to Stop DIYing and Call a Plumber for Toilet Installation Near You

YouTube has made a lot of homeowners more confident about DIY plumbing — and that's not a bad thing. Swapping a flapper valve is genuinely something most people can handle. But there's a category of toilet work where calling a licensed plumber is the smarter and safer play.

Call a Pro When:

  • You're installing a brand-new toilet (improper installation leads to leaks, rocking, and wax ring failure)
  • There's visible water damage around the base or under the floor
  • You suspect a clog beyond the toilet trap — it may be in the main drain line
  • The shut-off valve behind the toilet won't close completely
  • You're upgrading to a bidet toilet or smart toilet with electrical requirements
  • The toilet is in a tight space, upstairs bathroom, or involves subfloor work

Choosing a Replacement Toilet: What Elgin Homeowners Should Know

If you've decided replacement is the way to go, the market has genuinely improved. Today's toilets are quieter, more efficient, and far more comfortable than older models. Here are the features worth paying attention to:

  • WaterSense Certification: Look for the EPA WaterSense label. These toilets use 1.28 GPF or less and can save a household roughly 13,000 gallons per year.
  • Comfort Height (ADA): Also called "right height" toilets — the seat sits 17–19 inches from the floor, which is easier on knees and hips. Highly recommended for anyone over 50 or with mobility concerns.
  • Elongated vs. Round Bowl: Elongated bowls are more comfortable for most adults; round bowls are better for smaller bathrooms where space is tight.
  • Dual Flush: One flush setting for liquid waste (0.8 GPF), another for solid (1.6 GPF). A great middle ground if maximum water savings is a priority.
  • Rough-in Size: Most homes have a 12-inch rough-in (distance from wall to drain center). Measure before you buy — the wrong rough-in size means the toilet won't fit properly.

Pro tip from Fox Valley Plumbing: Don't buy a toilet from a big-box store and then call a plumber to install it without checking first. Some brands and models have known installation quirks. We're happy to recommend fixtures that we know install cleanly and last — just ask when you call.
